No other continent asks this much of you — or gives this much back. From the Caribbean heat of Colombia's walled city to the wind-scoured silence of Patagonia, this is a journey through altitude sickness, wonder, and the slow, humbling realization that South America is larger than any plan you made for it.

The Route

Six stops.
One entire continent.

01
Stop 01 · The Gateway

Cartagena, Colombia

A walled city the color of ripe fruit, built by conquistadors and claimed, eventually, by everyone else.

Cartagena is your entry point to the continent and an argument, all by itself, for the whole trip. The old walled city — a UNESCO site since 1984 — is a maze of bougainvillea-draped balconies, cobblestone plazas, and colonial churches painted in every shade the tropics can produce. Walk the Castillo de San Felipe at dusk. Eat arepas de huevo for breakfast. Let the Caribbean heat convince you that every subsequent journey will feel this effortless. It won't, but Cartagena earns the illusion.

Old City walls: 11 km of fortifications UNESCO World Heritage since 1984 Avg temp: 86°F / 30°C year-round Closest airport: Rafael Núñez (CTG) Don't miss: Castillo de San Felipe at sunset
✈️
Fly to Cusco via Bogotá or Lima
~6–9 hrs total travel · 2,600 mi / 4,200 km south
One of the more logistically real moments of the trip: there is no easy overland route. Fly. Then spend a full day at altitude in Cusco before attempting Machu Picchu — 11,150 ft will remind you it's there.
Worth the Detour
🏙️
Bogotá, Colombia
The Gold Museum alone justifies a layover. La Candelaria is colonial and gritty in equal measure.
🌊
Medellín, Colombia
A city that rewrote its own story. The cable cars over the hillside barrios are as moving as any museum.
🏔️
Cusco, Peru
Spend at least two nights acclimatizing. The Plaza de Armas is magnificent; the altitude is merciless.
02
Stop 02 · The Lost City

Machu Picchu, Peru

The photographs have not lied to you — but they have failed to convey the altitude, the cloud, and the feeling of standing somewhere that shouldn't exist.

There is no way to be blasé about Machu Picchu. The Inca citadel sits at 7,970 feet above sea level, wedged between two mountain peaks, surrounded on three sides by a sheer drop into cloud forest. The train from Cusco through the Sacred Valley is half the experience. Book the earliest entry slot to beat the crowds and the clouds; by 9am both tend to arrive. The hike up Huayna Picchu, if you can get a permit, changes the view entirely — and the view was already perfect.

Altitude: 7,970 ft / 2,430 m Built circa 1450, abandoned 1572 UNESCO + New Seven Wonders Access: train from Aguas Calientes Daily visitor cap: 5,600
🚌
Overland to Uyuni via Puno & La Paz
~2–3 days overland · Peru → Bolivia
This stretch is the real South America: overnight buses, border crossings at 12,000 ft, Lake Titicaca glittering at dawn. The journey from Cusco to Uyuni via Puno and La Paz is uncomfortable and utterly unforgettable. Flying is faster; overland is the point.
Worth the Detour
🚢
Lake Titicaca
The highest navigable lake on earth. The floating reed islands of the Uros people are unlike anything else on the continent.
🏔️
La Paz, Bolivia
The world's highest capital city at 11,942 ft. The cable car system over the city is both transport and tourist attraction.
💀
Death Road, Bolivia
The North Yungas Road is now a mountain biking route. Most operators run you downhill — still plenty terrifying.
03
Stop 03 · The Salt Flat

Salar de Uyuni, Bolivia

The largest mirror on earth. At 11,995 feet, with no horizon, the brain eventually gives up trying to orient itself.

The Salar is the world's largest salt flat — 4,050 square miles of white crust at impossibly high altitude, formed from prehistoric lakes that dried and left only minerals and silence behind. In the dry season it's a cracked white expanse stretching to nothing. In the wet season a thin layer of water turns it into a perfect mirror that reflects the sky so precisely you can't tell where ground ends and atmosphere begins. Take a multi-day 4WD tour; the Atacama border region has geysers, flamingo lagoons, and colored mineral lakes that look implausible even when you're standing in them.

Area: 4,050 sq mi / 10,582 sq km Altitude: 11,995 ft / 3,656 m Salt depth: up to 30 ft Best mirror effect: Dec–Mar Tours depart from Uyuni town
✈️
Fly to Puerto Iguazú via Buenos Aires
~4–5 hrs total · Bolivia → Argentina
From the altiplano back to jungle. Fly from Uyuni or La Paz to Buenos Aires, then a connecting domestic flight to Puerto Iguazú. The altitude drop — from nearly 12,000 ft to 820 ft — will feel like arriving on a different planet.
Worth the Detour
🌿
Amazon Basin, Peru/Brazil
A serious detour but worth it: Iquitos in Peru or Manaus in Brazil for river lodges and genuine jungle.
🍷
Mendoza, Argentina
The Malbec heartland, with the Andes as a backdrop. Add a night if your schedule allows.
🎭
Asunción, Paraguay
South America's forgotten capital — warm, slow, and genuinely off the tourist trail.
04
Stop 04 · The Falls

Iguazu Falls, Argentina

Eleanor Roosevelt reportedly saw these and said "Poor Niagara." Roosevelt was being generous to Niagara.

Iguazu is 1.7 miles wide and contains 275 individual falls — the largest of which, the Garganta del Diablo (Devil's Throat), drops 269 feet in a thundering U-shaped canyon that fills the air with mist visible from kilometers away. The Argentine side gets you closest; the Brazilian side gives you the panoramic view. Do both if you can — it's a short bus ride across the border and they are genuinely different experiences. The surrounding subtropical jungle is a national park on both sides and harbors toucans, coatis, and caiman along the riverbanks.

Width: 1.7 mi / 2.7 km 275 individual cataracts Devil's Throat: 269 ft / 82 m drop UNESCO Heritage on both sides Argentina: up close · Brazil: panoramic
✈️
Fly to Buenos Aires
~2 hrs · Puerto Iguazú → Ezeiza (EZE)
Short hop down to the capital. The bus is also a reasonable option — 18 hours overnight, and the Pampas are flat enough that you'll sleep through most of it.
Worth the Detour
🐄
The Pampas
Estancia day-trips from Buenos Aires for asado, gauchos, and the flattest land on earth. A total gear-change after the falls.
🎶
Colonia del Sacramento, Uruguay
A one-hour ferry from Buenos Aires to a perfectly preserved Portuguese colonial town. Good wine and slower WiFi.
🌊
Montevideo, Uruguay
Underrated, walkable, and genuinely charming. The Mercado del Puerto on a Sunday is one of the continent's great culinary experiences.
05
Stop 05 · The Capital of Everything

Buenos Aires, Argentina

Eight million people who eat dinner at midnight, take tango personally, and are correct about the steak.

Buenos Aires is where South America puts on its best clothes and refuses to go to bed. The city is vast, elegant, and built on a scale that makes European capitals feel provincial — wide Haussmann-influenced boulevards, enormous parks, neighborhoods each with their own personality and accent. Palermo for restaurants and galleries, San Telmo for tango and antiques, La Boca for color and football mythology. The asado culture alone justifies a long stay. Give it at least four days; it will feel insufficient.

Population: 15 million metro area Widest avenue on earth: 9 de Julio Second largest opera house in world Best months: Mar–May, Sep–Nov Don't miss: La Recoleta Cemetery at dusk
✈️
Fly to El Calafate or Punta Arenas
~3–4 hrs · Buenos Aires → Patagonia
The south is not casually reachable by land — Patagonia begins where the roads become suggestions. Fly to El Calafate for the Perito Moreno glacier, or Punta Arenas for Torres del Paine. Both are right. The wind will be waiting for you either way.
Worth the Detour
🐧
Punta Tombo, Argentina
The largest Magellanic penguin colony on the continent. A million birds, completely unimpressed by you.
🚢
Ushuaia, Tierra del Fuego
The southernmost city on earth. Ferries to Antarctica depart from here; just seeing the harbor in winter is enough.
🗺️
Bariloche, Argentina
Swiss-looking chocolate-town in the lake district, ringed by Andean peaks. Ski in winter, hike in summer.
06
Stop 06 · The End of the World

Patagonia, Argentina

The wind here is not weather. It is geography. It will push you sideways, make you laugh, and reset something in you that you didn't know needed resetting.

Patagonia is less a place than a condition — a vast, windswept region spanning southern Argentina and Chile, defined by granite spires, calving glaciers, and the particular silence of land that has never been crowded. The Perito Moreno Glacier, one of the few in the world that is not retreating, advances at a rate of 6 feet per day and periodically collapses into Lake Argentina in explosions of blue ice. Torres del Paine, across the Chilean border, is the continent's great hiking destination. The W Trek takes five days. The light here, at the bottom of the world, is unlike anywhere else.

Perito Moreno: advances ~6 ft per day Torres del Paine: W Trek 5 days Best season: Nov–Mar Winds regularly exceed 75 mph Fly out via Punta Arenas or El Calafate

Before you go.

The Altitude Question

Cusco, Uyuni, and Lake Titicaca all sit above 11,000 feet. Altitude sickness is real and doesn't discriminate by fitness level. Spend two nights in Cusco before heading to Machu Picchu. Avoid alcohol, drink water constantly, and consider acetazolamide if your doctor approves. Don't rush the high section of this route.

Flying vs. Overland

This route spans 7,700 miles. Bus travel between the major stops is possible but involves multi-day journeys on roads that range from superb to genuinely challenging. Budget travellers take the buses; everyone gets the story. The sweet spot is flying the longest hops (Cartagena–Cusco, Uyuni–Iguazú) and going overland where the journey is the point.

Best Time to Travel

There is no universally perfect season. The Salar de Uyuni's mirror effect requires the wet season (Dec–Mar). Patagonia is most accessible Nov–Mar. The Amazon is beautiful in wet season and navigable in dry. A 4–6 week trip will inevitably span two climate zones — dress in layers, pack light, and accept that somewhere will be the wrong season.

Currency and Cards

Argentina's currency situation requires research before you arrive — exchange rates fluctuate significantly and the gap between official and parallel rates has historically been dramatic. Bolivia and Peru are largely cash economies outside major cities. Carry US dollars as a backup; they are accepted or exchangeable everywhere on the continent.

Safety and Sense

South America's reputation is worse than its reality in most tourist areas, but basic precautions apply everywhere: use registered taxis or apps, don't flash expensive equipment, and trust your instincts. Cartagena and Buenos Aires have the most street-smart requirements; Patagonia and Uyuni are among the safest environments on the continent.

How Long You Really Need

Four weeks is the minimum to feel like you've arrived rather than just passed through. Six is the honest answer. Machu Picchu and the Salar alone deserve a week between them when you factor in transit and acclimatization. Patagonia will steal days you didn't budget. The continent rewards those who leave room in the itinerary for the itinerary to change.
