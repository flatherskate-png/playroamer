The American East Coast is the country's original spine — 1,500 miles of history, ambition, and Spanish moss running from New England's red brick and cold Atlantic to the Art Deco pastel of South Beach. Drive it south. The weather improves continuously and so does the food.

The Route

Seven stops.
One coast, top to bottom.

01
Stop 01 · The Beginning

Boston, MA

The city that started a revolution, then spent the next 250 years being quietly correct about everything.

Boston is America's most walkable city and one of its most layered — a place where a colonial graveyard sits next to a gleaming hospital, where the accent is its own cultural institution, and where the argument about whether the clam chowder is better here or in New York is not an argument anyone from New York is winning. Walk the Freedom Trail's 2.5 miles of red brick past 16 historic sites. Cross the Charles River to Cambridge and wander Harvard Yard. Eat lobster rolls on the waterfront. The city is compact enough to cover on foot in two days and deep enough to reward a week.

Freedom Trail: 2.5 mi, 16 sites Oldest public park in US: Boston Common, 1634 Population: 700,000 city / 4.9M metro Best months: Sep–Nov (foliage) Don't miss: Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum
3 hr 30 min southwest 215 mi · I-95 S or the scenic Post Road
Side Quests Along the Way
🦞
Cape Cod, MA
A worthwhile detour east before heading south. Provincetown at the tip is an artists' colony with spectacular light and good oysters.
⛵
Newport, Rhode Island
The Gilded Age mansions along Bellevue Avenue are preposterous in scale and fascinating in detail. The Breakers alone is worth the detour.
🍕
New Haven, CT
Home to Yale and, more importantly, to some of America's most contested pizza. Frank Pepe's has been making white clam pie since 1925. The argument about whether it's the best in the country is ongoing and delicious.
02
Stop 02 · The City

New York, NY

Eight million people who have collectively decided that this specific arrangement of concrete and ambition is the only place worth being. They are not entirely wrong.

New York resists summarizing. It is five boroughs, 8.3 million people, and more food options per square mile than anywhere else on earth. The obvious things — Central Park, the High Line, the Brooklyn Bridge at dawn, a slice from a counter place in the West Village — are obvious because they are genuinely excellent. Spend at least four days. Stay in Brooklyn if the budget allows it; Manhattan hotels are expensive and the subway brings you in faster than you'd expect. The Metropolitan Museum alone will absorb a full day if you let it. Leave time for the neighborhoods: the West Village, Chinatown, Astoria in Queens for Greek food and a slower pace.

Population: 8.3M city / 20M metro 24,000+ restaurants Central Park: 843 acres Subway: 472 stations Don't miss: Brooklyn Bridge at dawn
1 hr 30 min southwest 95 mi · I-95 S or NJ Turnpike
Side Quests Along the Way
🎡
Asbury Park, NJ
The Jersey Shore town that spawned Springsteen, recently revived. The boardwalk, the Stone Pony, a good craft beer scene.
🏛️
Princeton, NJ
A beautiful campus town worth a lunch stop. Einstein lived here. The art museum is free and unexpectedly world-class.
🦅
Valley Forge, PA
Washington's Continental Army wintered here in 1777–78. The national historical park is quiet, well-interpreted, and sobering.
03
Stop 03 · The Birthplace

Philadelphia, PA

Where the Declaration of Independence was signed, the cheesesteak was invented, and the locals have strong opinions about both.

Philadelphia is an underrated city that has been quietly excellent for decades while everyone drove past it on the way to New York. The historic district around Independence Hall is genuinely moving — the Liberty Bell, the Constitution Center, the cobblestoned streets of Elfreth's Alley (America's oldest continuously inhabited residential street, in use since 1703). The Philadelphia Museum of Art has one of the country's great collections and the Rocky steps outside are obligatory regardless of how you feel about it. The Reading Terminal Market for breakfast. A cheesesteak from Pat's or Geno's for lunch. The debate about which one is better is the correct introduction to the city.

Independence Hall: built 1753 Elfreth's Alley: continuously inhabited since 1703 Philadelphia Museum of Art: 240,000 objects Population: 1.6M city Don't miss: Reading Terminal Market
2 hr south 140 mi · I-95 S
Side Quests Along the Way
⚓
Annapolis, MD
The sailing capital of America — a colonial waterfront town with the oldest State House in continuous use in the US. Good crab cakes on the dock.
🦀
Baltimore, MD
The Inner Harbor, the Edgar Allan Poe House, and a whole city built around blue crab. Fells Point for bars. The American Visionary Art Museum for something genuinely strange.
🌸
Gettysburg, PA
A short detour west. The Civil War battlefield where 51,000 casualties occurred over three days in 1863. The auto tour is thoughtful; the silence is total.
04
Stop 04 · The Capital

Washington, DC

A city designed on a grid by a French engineer to project power, which it does, relentlessly, from every marble cornice.

Washington is the only major American city that was planned from scratch, and it shows — grand diagonal avenues cutting across the grid, monuments placed at sightlines, the Mall stretching two miles from the Capitol to the Lincoln Memorial. The Smithsonian museums are all free and collectively contain the greatest trove of objects in the Western Hemisphere. Budget a full day just for the National Museum of Natural History and the National Air and Space Museum. The National Mall at dawn, before the tour groups arrive, is one of the most quietly powerful experiences in America. Georgetown for dinner; U Street for live music.

The Mall: 2 miles, Capitol to Lincoln Memorial 19 free Smithsonian museums National Archives: original Constitution on display Best months: Mar–Apr (cherry blossoms) Don't miss: Lincoln Memorial at dawn
8 hr 30 min south 510 mi · I-95 S · the longest stretch of the drive
Side Quests Along the Way
🏇
Richmond, VA
Virginia's capital has one of the best craft beer scenes on the East Coast and a Civil War history told honestly at the American Civil War Museum.
🌊
Outer Banks, NC
A thin barrier island chain off North Carolina with wild horses, the Wright Brothers Memorial at Kitty Hawk, and beaches that feel genuinely remote.
🌿
Wilmington, NC
A pretty river port with antebellum architecture and easy access to Cape Fear beaches. A good overnight break on the long DC–Charleston haul.
05
Stop 05 · The Lowcountry

Charleston, SC

Pastel row houses on cobblestone streets, sweetgrass baskets in the market, and a cuisine that has been quietly ahead of the rest of America for three hundred years.

Charleston is one of the most beautiful cities in America and it knows it, which is the only mildly irritating thing about it. The historic district — a peninsula of antebellum architecture, Rainbow Row, and white-steepled churches draped in Spanish moss — is extraordinarily well preserved and genuinely lovely to walk. The food scene is exceptional: Lowcountry cooking built on rice, shellfish, and spice, served in restaurants that range from white-tablecloth to counter stools. Fort Sumter, where the Civil War began in April 1861, sits in the harbor a short ferry ride away. The city's history is gorgeous and brutal in equal measure; the International African American Museum, opened in 2023, addresses the second half of that sentence directly.

Founded: 1670 — oldest city in the Southeast Fort Sumter: Civil War began here, Apr 12, 1861 IAAM opened 2023 on original slave trade site Best months: Mar–May, Sep–Nov Don't miss: Rainbow Row at golden hour
1 hr 45 min south 107 mi · I-95 S to I-16 E
Side Quests Along the Way
🐊
Hilton Head Island, SC
Marsh, beach, and bike paths on a sea island. Considerably quieter than Charleston and genuinely beautiful in the low-angle afternoon light.
🌳
Beaufort, SC
A small Lowcountry town with antebellum homes draped in live oaks. The filming location for The Big Chill, Forrest Gump, and The Great Santini.
🦅
Cumberland Island, GA
A roadless barrier island accessible only by ferry, with wild horses on the beach and the ruins of the Carnegie family's Dungeness mansion in the dunes.
06
Stop 06 · The Garden City

Savannah, GA

Twenty-two squares of live oaks draped in Spanish moss, a city grid that has barely changed since 1733, and a general attitude that things will happen when they happen.

Savannah operates at a pace that makes Charleston feel brisk. The city was laid out in 1733 on a grid of squares — each a small park canopied by ancient live oaks dripping with Spanish moss — and that original plan remains almost perfectly intact, making it one of the best-preserved colonial cities in America. Walk every square. The Mercer Williams House. Bonaventure Cemetery, which is genuinely one of the most beautiful cemeteries in the world. The Forsyth Park fountain. Leopold's Ice Cream, open since 1919. River Street for a drink and the view. Savannah is an argument for slow travel delivered entirely in Spanish moss and bourbon.

Founded: 1733 — Georgia's first city 22 original squares, 21 survive Bonaventure Cemetery: National Historic Landmark Leopold's Ice Cream: open since 1919 Don't miss: Forsyth Park at dusk
6 hr south 390 mi · I-95 S
Side Quests Along the Way
🚀
Kennedy Space Center, FL
The launch complex on Merritt Island where Apollo, the Space Shuttle, and current SpaceX missions depart. A full day, easily. The Saturn V display hall is enormous and moving.
🐢
Amelia Island, FL
A quieter Florida barrier island just south of the Georgia border with Victorian architecture and loggerhead sea turtle nesting beaches.
🌴
St. Augustine, FL
The oldest continuously occupied European settlement in the US, founded by Spain in 1565. The Castillo de San Marcos fort still stands on the water.
07
Stop 07 · Journey's End

Miami, FL

The city where the road ends, the Atlantic begins, and everyone is dressed better than you.

Miami is the logical conclusion of the East Coast — a city built from scratch in the 20th century that has become one of the most culturally distinct places in America, neither fully North nor South, pulling as strongly toward Latin America and the Caribbean as toward the mainland above it. South Beach's Art Deco Historic District is a mile of pastel 1930s architecture that functions as an outdoor museum and a very loud nightlife corridor simultaneously. The Wynwood Walls for street art. Little Havana for a cortadito and a domino game in Maximo Gomez Park. The Everglades, an hour west, for an airboat and the realization that the city is essentially sitting on a river of grass. You made it. The water is warm.

Art Deco District: 800+ protected buildings 800,000+ Spanish speakers in metro area Everglades: 30 min west by car Best months: Nov–Apr (dry season) Don't miss: Wynwood Walls on a weekday morning

Before you go.

The Drive vs. The Train

I-95 is the obvious spine and perfectly functional, but the Amtrak Auto Train runs from Lorton, Virginia (just south of DC) direct to Sanford, Florida, carrying you and your car overnight in a single trip. It's slower on paper and faster in practice — you sleep while covering 855 miles, and you arrive in Florida with a car and no road fatigue. Book it early; it sells out.

The Long Middle

The DC-to-Charleston stretch is the longest drive on the route at around 8.5 hours. Break it in Richmond (an underrated overnight) or the Outer Banks if the season is right. Don't try to cover it in one push unless you're genuinely committed to arriving exhausted.

Seasons

Spring (March–May) is ideal for the whole route: the cherry blossoms in DC, mild temperatures through the Carolinas, and pre-summer prices. Fall is equally good from Boston to DC. Florida is best November through April — the humidity and hurricane season (June–November) make summer a manageable but sweaty proposition in Miami.

Pacing

Two weeks is the minimum to avoid sprinting. Three is comfortable. New York will steal days you budgeted for Philadelphia, Charleston will hold you longer than planned, and Savannah makes leaving difficult. The DC-to-Charleston drive is the one you'll want to break up. Build slack into the southern half; it has a way of slowing you down.

Where to Stay

New York: Brooklyn or Lower Manhattan for value and access. DC: Capitol Hill or Dupont Circle for walkability. Charleston: stay in the historic district — the atmosphere is the point. Savannah: the squares area, nowhere else. Miami: South Beach for the Art Deco experience, Wynwood for something quieter and cooler. Book ahead for Charleston and Savannah in spring.

What to Eat, State by State

Boston: chowder and lobster rolls. New York: everything, but especially a slice and a bagel. Philadelphia: cheesesteak and the Reading Terminal. DC: Ethiopian food on 9th Street NW. Charleston: shrimp and grits and anything at Husk. Savannah: low-country boil and Leopold's ice cream. Miami: Cuban food in Little Havana and stone crab claws in season (Oct–May).
