For seven centuries, Islamic civilisation shaped the Iberian Peninsula — its architecture, its food, its gardens, its mathematics. This route follows that inheritance from the grand avenues of Madrid, down through the tile-faced city of Lisbon, across the Strait of Gibraltar, and deep into Morocco until the road runs out at the edge of the Sahara.

The Route

Six stops.
Two continents, one inheritance.

01
Stop 01 · The Capital

Madrid, Spain

A city that eats dinner at 10pm, closes nothing before midnight, and has somehow built three of the world's great art museums within walking distance of each other.

Madrid sits at the geographic centre of the Iberian Peninsula — the highest capital city in the European Union — and operates with a confidence that comes from knowing it has been Spain's beating heart since 1561. The Golden Triangle of Art brings together the Prado, the Reina Sofía, and the Thyssen-Bornemisza within a few minutes' walk: Velázquez and Goya at the first, Picasso's Guernica at the second, and everything else at the third. Wander the Retiro park at noon. Eat pintxos in La Latina. Madrid rewards staying up late and sleeping late; adjust your schedule accordingly and the city opens completely.

Elevation: 2,188 ft / 667 m — highest EU capital Prado: 8,000 paintings in the collection Retiro Park: 350 acres in the city centre Best months: Apr–Jun, Sep–Oct Don't miss: Guernica at Reina Sofía
6 hr west 390 mi · A-5 motorway to Lisbon
Side Quests Along the Way
🏰
Toledo, Spain
An hour south of Madrid, a medieval walled city on a granite hill where Christian, Muslim, and Jewish cultures coexisted for centuries. El Greco lived and painted here. Half a day minimum.
🦅
Mérida, Spain
Roman Emerita Augusta — the best-preserved Roman theatre in Spain, still used for performances in summer. The archaeological museum is genuinely world-class.
🍷
Évora, Portugal
A walled Alentejo town with a Roman temple, a Gothic cathedral, and a bone chapel that is exactly as unsettling as it sounds. Portugal's quietest great city.
02
Stop 02 · The Atlantic City

Lisbon, Portugal

Seven hills, a river that looks like an ocean, pastel tiles on every surface, and a melancholy so beautiful it has its own word: saudade.

Lisbon is the westernmost capital in continental Europe and has the feel of a city that reached the edge of the known world and simply stopped, unhurried, facing the Atlantic. The Alfama district — the old Moorish quarter, its streets too steep and narrow for cars — is the city's emotional heart, best explored without a map. Take tram 28 for the view, then abandon it and walk. The Jerónimos Monastery in Belém is the finest example of Manueline architecture on earth — a style born from the wealth of the Age of Discoveries, encrusted with ropes, armillary spheres, and exotic naturalistic detail. Eat a pastel de nata warm from the oven at Pastéis de Belém, open since 1837. Stay three days.

Alfama: the original Moorish medina, 8th century Jerónimos Monastery: UNESCO, built 1501 Pastel de nata: the recipe is a secret since 1837 Best months: Apr–Jun, Sep–Oct Don't miss: Miradouro da Graça at sunset
6 hr 30 min southeast 390 mi · A-2 / E-1 south to Seville, then A-92 to Granada
Side Quests Along the Way
💃
Seville, Spain
The Alcázar palace, the Cathedral (the world's largest Gothic church), and a flamenco tablao after 10pm. Worth a night of its own between Lisbon and Granada.
🌿
Córdoba, Spain
The Mezquita — a hypostyle mosque of 856 columns with a Catholic cathedral built directly inside it — is one of the most extraordinary buildings in Europe. The Jewish quarter around it is intact and beautiful.
🏖️
Algarve Coast, Portugal
A detour south before crossing into Spain. The sea stacks and grottos around Lagos and Sagres are Portugal's most dramatic coastline.
03
Stop 03 · The Palace

Alhambra, Granada, Spain

The last great Moorish palace in Europe, built on a red hill above a city the Nasrid sultans refused to leave — until, in 1492, they had no choice.

The Alhambra is the finest surviving example of Moorish palatial architecture in the world, and it demands more time than most visitors give it. The Nasrid Palaces — the ceremonial heart of the complex — are a sustained act of architectural genius: rooms of breathtaking geometric tilework giving way to carved stucco muqarnas ceilings, water channels running through marble courtyards, and the Court of the Lions with its famous fountain supported by twelve marble lions. The Generalife gardens above are what the sultans retreated to when the palace felt like work. Book timed-entry tickets weeks in advance; they sell out entirely in peak season and the evening sessions, lit differently, are worth seeking out.

Built: 13th–14th century, Nasrid dynasty UNESCO World Heritage since 1984 Timed entry: book weeks in advance Evening sessions available: different light entirely Don't miss: Mirador de San Nicolás view at dusk
🚢
Drive to Tarifa, ferry to Tangier
2 hr drive south · then 35 min ferry crossing · Spain → Morocco
The Strait of Gibraltar crossing is one of the great short journeys — you watch Europe recede and Africa approach in less than an hour. Tarifa is the southernmost point of continental Europe; on a clear day you can see Morocco from the beach. The ferry to Tangier Med departs regularly. Have your passport ready; this is a real border crossing.
Side Quests Along the Way
🦅
Gibraltar
The British Overseas Territory at the tip of Spain, with Barbary macaques on the Rock, a cable car, and a surreal Anglo-Spanish cultural overlap that shouldn't work but does.
🌊
Tarifa, Spain
The windiest town in Europe and the kitesurfing capital of the continent. The old walled medina is quiet and the beach stretches for miles.
🏛️
Tangier, Morocco
The port city that has always been a threshold — between Europe and Africa, between languages, between centuries. The Kasbah Museum and the Medina are the right entry point to Morocco.
04
Stop 04 · The Red City

Marrakech, Morocco

A city that assaults every sense simultaneously and makes you feel, somehow, that this is exactly right.

Marrakech is not subtle. The medina is a dense, labyrinthine world of souks, riads, mosques, and tanneries that operates at a pace and a volume entirely its own. The Djemaa el-Fna — the great central square — transforms from an afternoon market of orange juice stalls and fortune tellers into an evening theatre of food smoke, musicians, storytellers, and snake charmers. The Bahia Palace shows what Moroccan domestic architecture achieves at its most ambitious. The Saadian Tombs, sealed for centuries and only rediscovered in 1917. Stay in a riad in the medina: the contrast between the chaos outside and the cool, tiled interior courtyard is the essential Marrakech experience.

Medina: UNESCO World Heritage since 1985 Djemaa el-Fna: UNESCO Intangible Heritage Founded: 1070 by the Almoravid dynasty Best months: Oct–Apr Don't miss: Saadian Tombs, Bahia Palace
3 hr north 185 mi · A7 motorway to Fez
Side Quests Along the Way
🔵
Chefchaouen, Morocco
The blue city in the Rif Mountains — every wall, stair, and doorway painted in shades of indigo and cerulean. More beautiful and less crowded than Marrakech.
🌊
Essaouira, Morocco
A walled coastal medina on the Atlantic, battered by constant wind and consequently much cooler than Marrakech. Jimi Hendrix was briefly a regular. Good fresh fish.
🏔️
Atlas Mountains
The high Atlas between Marrakech and the desert. Toubkal, at 13,671 ft, is North Africa's highest peak. The Ourika Valley is an easier day-trip from Marrakech.
05
Stop 05 · The Medieval City

Fez, Morocco

The world's largest car-free urban zone, where the medieval city is not a preserved relic but a living, working, tanning, dyeing, baking reality.

Fez el-Bali is the world's oldest continuously inhabited medieval city — a UNESCO-listed labyrinth of 9,000 streets and alleyways that has operated without fundamental change since the 9th century. The medina is genuinely disorienting: a maze where every turn reveals a craftsman at work, a medersa courtyard, or a mosque entrance you're not allowed to enter. The Chouara Tannery, seen from the leather shop terraces above, is one of Morocco's iconic images — round stone vats of dye in earthy reds, ochres, and blues, worked by hand as they have been for a thousand years. Hire a local guide; the medina requires one, and the best guides reveal layers the maps cannot show.

Fez el-Bali: UNESCO, founded 789 AD 9,000 streets in the medina University of al-Qarawiyyin: oldest in the world, 859 AD Chouara Tannery: operating since 11th century Guide strongly recommended
7 hr southeast 360 mi · N13 over the Middle Atlas to Merzouga · the most dramatic drive of the route
Side Quests Along the Way
🌹
Valley of Roses, Dadès
The Dadès and Draa valleys bloom with rose fields every May, harvested for the global perfume industry. The kasbahs along this stretch are extraordinary.
🎬
Ouarzazate, Morocco
The "Hollywood of Africa" — Lawrence of Arabia, Gladiator, and Game of Thrones all filmed here. The Atlas Corporation Studios are open for tours. The Aït Benhaddou ksar is UNESCO-listed.
🦎
Todra Gorge
A 300-metre-high slot canyon cut through rose-red limestone, narrow enough in places to touch both walls. Rock climbers come from Europe for the routes; hikers come for the light at noon.
06
Stop 06 · The Sahara

Erg Chebbi, Morocco

The road ends at Merzouga and the dunes begin — some rising to 500 feet, shifting imperceptibly, the colour of a lit match at dusk.

Erg Chebbi is an isolated sea of Saharan sand dunes near the Algerian border, rising abruptly from the flat desert plains around Merzouga to heights of 500 feet. It is not the largest erg in the Sahara — that would be the Erg Chech in Algeria — but it is the most accessible from Morocco and among the most visually dramatic. The light here changes every hour. At midday the sand is almost white; by late afternoon it deepens through amber to a vivid orange-red that photographers chase from across the world. Take a camel trek into the dunes at sunset and spend the night in a desert camp — cold in winter, hot in summer, and worth it entirely. Wake before dawn. The silence before sunrise at the edge of the Sahara is the end point this whole route has been building toward.

Dune height: up to 500 ft / 150 m Base town: Merzouga, 35 km from Algerian border Best season: Oct–Apr Summer highs: 104°F / 40°C+ Don't miss: camel trek + overnight desert camp

Before you go.

The Strait Crossing

The ferry from Tarifa to Tangier Med is the route's pivot point — short in distance, significant in shift. Most nationalities need only a valid passport for Morocco (no visa for stays under 90 days for EU, US, UK, and many others). Check your specific nationality. The crossing itself takes 35 minutes on a good day; the border formalities on both sides add time. Allow two hours total.

Driving in Morocco

The road from Fez to Merzouga is long and crosses the Middle Atlas and pre-Saharan plateau — dramatic, mostly two-lane, and entirely manageable in a standard car. A 4WD is not required unless you plan to leave the tarmac around the dunes. Driving in Marrakech and Fez medinas is essentially impossible; park outside and walk. GPS works but local knowledge is better in the souks.

Best Season

Spring (March–May) and autumn (September–November) are ideal for the whole route. Marrakech and Fez in July and August are genuinely punishing — 100°F+ is common. The Sahara in winter is cold at night (freezing at altitude) but crisp and clear by day, which is actually the best time to visit Erg Chebbi. Madrid and Lisbon are comfortable year-round.

Alhambra Tickets

Book the Alhambra the moment your dates are fixed — ideally six to eight weeks in advance for peak season. The Nasrid Palaces section has a strict timed entry and sells out completely. Evening tickets are slightly easier to obtain and give you the rooms in a different, dimmer light that some prefer. The website is the only reliable booking channel; third-party resellers charge significant premiums.

Money in Morocco

The Moroccan dirham cannot be exported, so exchange on arrival and use what you carry. Cash is king in the medinas, souks, and desert camps — cards are accepted in Marrakech and Fez hotels and some larger restaurants, but not reliably elsewhere. Bargaining is expected in the souks: the first price quoted is the opening position, not the destination. Remain cheerful throughout.

How Long You Really Need

Two weeks covers the route at a pace that leaves you wanting more time everywhere. Three weeks is comfortable. The Fez-to-Merzouga drive alone is a day you don't want to rush — the landscape through the Middle Atlas and the Todra Gorge area earns its own overnight stop. Build in a rest day in Marrakech; the medina is its own full-day activity every day you're there.
